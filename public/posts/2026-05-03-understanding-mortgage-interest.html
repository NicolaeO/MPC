Understanding Mortgage Interest

A mortgage is likely the largest financial commitment of your life — and the interest you pay on top of the loan can easily exceed the price of the home itself. Understanding exactly how it works lets you make smarter decisions about loan terms, down payments, and when to pay extra.

What Is a Mortgage?

A mortgage is a loan used to purchase real estate, secured by the property itself. If you stop making payments, the lender can take the home through foreclosure. You make fixed monthly payments over a set term — typically 15 or 30 years — until the loan is paid off.

Your monthly payment covers two things: principal (repaying the amount you borrowed) and interest (the cost of borrowing). Early in the loan, nearly all of each payment is interest. Over time, the balance shifts toward principal. This structure is called amortization.

Fixed-Rate vs. Adjustable-Rate Mortgages

Fixed-Rate

Your interest rate is locked in for the entire loan term. Your principal + interest payment never changes.


Best for: People who want predictability and plan to stay in the home long-term.


Common terms: 30-year (lower monthly payment, more total interest) and 15-year (higher payment, dramatically less interest overall).

Adjustable-Rate (ARM)

Starts with a fixed rate for an initial period (commonly 5, 7, or 10 years), then adjusts annually based on a market index.


Best for: People confident they will sell or refinance before the adjustment period begins.


Risk: If rates rise significantly before you sell, your payment can jump by hundreds of dollars per month.

FHA / VA / USDA

FHA: Government-backed, down payment as low as 3.5%, accessible with credit scores from 580. Requires mortgage insurance premium (MIP) for life of loan.


VA: For US military veterans and service members. No down payment required, no PMI.


USDA: For rural properties, no down payment required for qualifying buyers.

How Mortgage Interest Is Calculated

Mortgage interest is calculated on the remaining loan balance each month. The formula:

Monthly Interest = Remaining Balance × (Annual Rate ÷ 12)

Monthly Payment = P × [ r(1+r)^n ] ÷ [ (1+r)^n − 1 ]

  P = loan principal
  r = monthly rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
  n = total number of payments (years × 12)

Real example — $400,000 loan at 7% for 30 years:

  • Monthly rate: 7% ÷ 12 = 0.5833%
  • Number of payments: 30 × 12 = 360
  • Monthly P&I payment: $2,661
  • Total paid over 30 years: $2,661 × 360 = $957,960
  • Total interest paid: $957,960 − $400,000 = $557,960
You pay more in interest than the home itself costs. On a $400,000 loan at 7% over 30 years, you hand the bank $557,960 in interest alone. This is why the interest rate and loan term matter so much.

The Amortization Schedule: Why Early Payments Are Mostly Interest

Even though your $2,661 monthly payment never changes, what that money covers shifts dramatically over time. Because interest is calculated on the remaining balance, and the balance is largest at the beginning, the interest portion dominates early payments.

Here is how the breakdown changes on that same $400,000 / 7% / 30-year loan:

Payment # Month / Year Interest Portion Principal Portion Remaining Balance
1 Month 1 $2,333 $328 $399,672
60 Year 5 $2,216 $445 $380,460

180 Year 15 $1,773 $888 $303,879

360 Year 30 $15 $2,646 $0

Notice: after 10 years of payments, you have paid roughly $186,000 toward the mortgage — but your balance has only dropped from $400,000 to $349,553. You have only reduced the principal by $50,447. The rest went to interest.

30-Year vs. 15-Year: The Real Cost Difference

Loan Rate Monthly Payment Total Interest Paid
$400,000 — 30-year fixed 7.00% $2,661 $557,960
$400,000 — 15-year fixed 6.50% $3,488 $227,840

Choosing a 15-year mortgage costs $827 more per month — but saves $330,120 in interest over the life of the loan and builds equity twice as fast. If you can afford it, the 15-year is almost always the better financial decision.

What Determines Your Interest Rate?

  • Credit score — the single biggest factor. A 760+ score gets you the best rates. Going from a 650 to a 750 can save 0.5–1.0% on your rate, which is tens of thousands of dollars over 30 years.
  • Down payment — larger down payment = lower rate + no PMI
  • Loan term — 15-year rates are typically 0.5–0.75% lower than 30-year rates
  • Loan type — conventional, FHA, VA, jumbo loans each have different rate structures
  • Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) — lenders want your total monthly debt payments below 43% of gross income
  • Market conditions — the Federal Reserve's interest rate policy and bond markets set the floor for mortgage rates nationwide
  • Property type and use — investment properties and second homes carry higher rates than primary residences

Mortgage Points: Paying to Lower Your Rate

You can pay discount points upfront at closing to buy a lower interest rate. One point = 1% of the loan amount and typically reduces your rate by about 0.25%.

Example: On a $400,000 loan, 1 point costs $4,000 and reduces your rate from 7.00% to 6.75%. Your payment drops from $2,661 to $2,594 — saving $67/month. Break-even: $4,000 ÷ $67 = 60 months (5 years). If you stay longer than 5 years, points save money. If you sell or refinance sooner, they do not.

When to buy points: Only if you are confident you will keep the loan past the break-even date. In a volatile rate environment where refinancing is likely, paying points is usually not worth it.

PMI: The Cost of a Small Down Payment

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is required on conventional loans when your down payment is less than 20%. It protects the lender — not you — if you default. PMI typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of the loan amount per year.

Example: On a $400,000 loan with a 10% down payment ($360,000 borrowed), PMI at 1% = $3,600/year = $300 extra per month added to your payment — on top of principal, interest, taxes, and insurance.

PMI is not permanent. Once your loan balance drops to 80% of the home's original value, you can request cancellation. At 78% it must be cancelled automatically by law (Homeowners Protection Act).

Strategy: If you are close to 20% down, it may be worth waiting to save more and avoid PMI entirely. If your home appreciates significantly, you can also request a new appraisal to prove you have reached 20% equity and eliminate PMI early.

How to Save on Mortgage Interest

Pay Extra Principal

Adding just $200/month in extra principal on a $400,000/7%/30-year loan saves over $100,000 in interest and cuts 5+ years off the loan. Specify that extra payments go to principal only.

Refinance When Rates Drop

If rates fall 0.75–1%+ below your current rate, refinancing can save thousands. Factor in closing costs (typically 2–3% of the loan). Break-even is usually 2–4 years — make sure you plan to stay.

Improve Credit Before Applying

Spending 6–12 months paying down debt and fixing credit report errors before applying can raise your score enough to qualify for a significantly better rate. On a large loan, 0.5% lower rate saves $50,000+ over 30 years.

Your Full Monthly Payment: More Than Just P&I

Lenders often quote only the principal and interest (P&I) payment. Your actual monthly housing cost includes:

  • Principal + Interest (P&I) — the mortgage payment itself
  • Property taxes — collected monthly and held in escrow; varies widely by location (0.5%–2.5% of home value per year)
  • Homeowners insurance — typically $100–$200/month
  • PMI — if down payment is under 20%
  • HOA fees — if applicable (condos, some neighborhoods)
Rule of thumb: Budget for total monthly housing costs (PITI) of no more than 28% of your gross monthly income. On a $120,000/year salary ($10,000/month), that means keeping total housing costs under $2,800/month.

The Takeaway

A mortgage is not just a loan — it is a long-term interest calculation that rewards borrowers who understand it. A higher credit score, a larger down payment, a shorter loan term, and occasional extra principal payments can save you hundreds of thousands of dollars. Before you sign, run the numbers, compare at least 3 lenders, and know exactly what you are committing to for the next 15 to 30 years.
