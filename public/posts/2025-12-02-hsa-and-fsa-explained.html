HSA and FSA Explained

The US healthcare system is expensive, but two special accounts — HSA and FSA — let you pay for medical expenses with pre-tax dollars, effectively giving you a 20–35% discount on everything from doctor visits to prescriptions.

HSA

Health Savings Account

Who can use it: Only available if you are enrolled in a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP).


2024 limits: $4,150 individual / $8,300 family


Key advantage: Triple tax benefit — contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-free, withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free.


Rolls over: Yes, indefinitely. Unused money stays in the account year after year.


Portable: You own it — not your employer. It stays with you when you change jobs.

FSA

Flexible Spending Account

Who can use it: Available with most employer health plans (not just HDHPs).


2024 limit: $3,200 per year


Key advantage: Contributions are pre-tax; full balance is available from January 1st, even before you have contributed it all.


Use-it-or-lose-it: Unused funds typically expire at year end. Up to $640 may roll over to the following year if your employer allows it.


Tied to employer: If you leave your job, you lose access to unused funds.

What Expenses Qualify?

Both accounts cover a wide range of IRS-approved medical expenses:

  • Doctor and specialist visits (your share of the cost)
  • Prescription medications
  • Dental care — cleanings, fillings, orthodontics
  • Vision care — glasses, contact lenses, eye exams
  • Over-the-counter medications (after the CARES Act, no prescription required)
  • Mental health therapy and counseling
  • Medical equipment and first aid supplies
  • Menstrual care products
Not covered: Cosmetic procedures, gym memberships, teeth whitening, and premiums for health insurance itself do not qualify (with some exceptions).

Dependent Care FSA

Separate from the healthcare FSA, a Dependent Care FSA lets you set aside up to $5,000 per year (per household) pre-tax to pay for childcare costs — daycare, after-school programs, and summer camps for children under age 13. This is a significant benefit for parents.

HSA as a Long-Term Investment

Here is an advanced strategy: once your HSA balance exceeds a threshold (often $1,000–$2,000), many providers let you invest the excess in index funds. Since HSA money rolls over forever and grows tax-free, you can treat it as a stealth retirement account specifically for healthcare costs in retirement — which are typically one of the largest expenses for retirees.

Strategy: If you are healthy, pay medical expenses out of pocket, save the receipts, and let your HSA grow invested. You can reimburse yourself for those same expenses years later — there is no time limit on reimbursements. This turns the HSA into a powerful tax-free investment vehicle.

Which One Should You Choose?

If your employer offers an HDHP with an HSA and you are generally healthy, the HSA is usually the better long-term choice due to the rollover and investment features. If you have predictable, high medical costs, an FSA with a lower-deductible plan may cost less overall. Run the numbers during your employer's open enrollment period.
