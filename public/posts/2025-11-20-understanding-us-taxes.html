Understanding US Taxes

The US tax system can feel overwhelming when you first arrive, but once you understand the basics you will realize it is manageable. This guide covers everything a new professional needs to know to stay compliant and avoid surprises.

How the Tax System Works

The US uses a progressive tax system — you pay a higher rate only on income above each bracket threshold, not on your entire income. Federal tax brackets for 2024 range from 10% to 37%, but most people in the middle class pay an effective rate far lower than their top bracket.

Beyond federal taxes, most states have their own income tax. A few states — Florida, Texas, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, South Dakota, and Alaska — have no state income tax at all.

The Two Most Common Tax Forms

W-2

Issued by your employer each January. Shows your total wages and taxes already withheld throughout the year. Most full-time employees get a W-2.

1099-NEC

Issued to freelancers and contractors. No taxes are withheld — you are responsible for paying them yourself. You may also receive 1099-INT for bank interest and 1099-DIV for dividends.

The Standard Deduction

Before calculating your taxes, the IRS lets you subtract a fixed amount from your income called the standard deduction. For 2024 it is $14,600 for single filers and $29,200 for married filing jointly. Most people take the standard deduction rather than itemizing.

Example: You earn $65,000. After the $14,600 standard deduction, your taxable income is $50,400. Only that amount is used to calculate your tax — not the full $65,000.

Filing Deadlines

  • April 15 — standard deadline to file and pay taxes
  • October 15 — extended deadline if you file Form 4868 by April 15 (extension gives more time to file, not to pay)
  • Quarterly deadlines — if you are self-employed or have significant non-wage income, you must pay estimated taxes four times per year (April, June, September, January)
Important: Filing late without an extension results in a penalty of 5% per month of unpaid taxes. Even if you cannot pay in full, file on time to minimize penalties.

Free Filing Resources

  • IRS Free File — free software if your income is under $79,000 (irs.gov/freefile)
  • VITA (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance) — free in-person tax help for people earning under ~$67,000, immigrants, and non-English speakers
  • FreeTaxUSA — free federal return, small fee for state
  • TurboTax / H&R Block — paid but user-friendly, free tier for simple returns

Tips for Immigrants

  • Your residency status for tax purposes (resident alien vs non-resident alien) affects which forms you file. Most immigrants on a green card or who pass the Substantial Presence Test file as resident aliens using Form 1040 — the same as US citizens.
  • Report foreign bank accounts if the total exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year (FBAR — FinCEN Form 114). This is separate from your tax return.
  • You may still owe taxes to Moldova or another country. Check if a tax treaty exists between the US and your home country to avoid double taxation.
  • Keep records of all income documents, receipts for deductible expenses, and filed returns for at least 3 years.

The Bottom Line

Taxes are not something to fear — they are a straightforward obligation once you know what to expect. File on time, take the standard deduction, and use free resources like VITA if you need help.
