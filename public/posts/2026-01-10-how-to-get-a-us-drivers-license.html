How to Get a US Driver's License

In most of the US, having a car is not a luxury — it is a necessity. Getting a driver's license is one of the first practical milestones after arriving, and while the process takes a few visits to the DMV, it is very manageable once you know what to expect.

Can You Drive on Your Foreign License?

If you are visiting or newly arrived, your foreign driver's license is generally valid for a short period (varies by state — typically 30–90 days or while your visa remains valid as a tourist). However, once you establish residency, you are required to obtain a state license.

An International Driving Permit (IDP) is a translation of your foreign license and is useful as a supplement, but it is not a standalone document — your foreign license must accompany it.

REAL ID vs. Standard License

A REAL ID-compliant license has a gold star in the corner and is accepted for domestic air travel and federal facilities. A standard license works for driving but not for boarding domestic flights as your ID. Get the REAL ID version when possible — it costs the same.

What Documents You Need

Requirements vary by state, but most DMVs ask for:

  • Proof of identity — passport with valid visa (accepted in all states)
  • Proof of lawful presence — I-94, visa, green card, or EAD card
  • Social Security Number — most states require it; bring your SSN card or document showing the number. If ineligible, some states accept an SSN ineligibility letter from the SSA.
  • Two proofs of state residency — utility bill, bank statement, lease agreement, or employer letter with your address (must show your name and current address)
Illinois, California, New York, and Washington allow driver's licenses regardless of immigration status and do not require an SSN. If you live in one of these states, check your state's specific requirements.

The Step-by-Step Process

1. Study

Download your state's DMV driver handbook (free online). Study traffic signs, rules, and laws. Take free online practice tests — most states offer them on the official DMV website.

2. Schedule

Book an appointment online at your state DMV website. Walk-ins are usually accepted but waits can be 2–3 hours. Appointments cut the wait to 15–30 minutes.

3. Knowledge Test

Multiple choice test, usually 20–40 questions. You need ~70–80% to pass. If you fail, you can typically retake it after a waiting period (1–3 days).

4. Driving Test

Schedule separately after passing the knowledge test. You bring a vehicle (insured). Examiner rides with you for ~15–20 minutes testing basic maneuvers, parking, and road rules.

5. Get Your License

Pay the fee ($15–$40 typically). Receive a temporary paper license immediately. Your plastic card arrives by mail in 7–14 days.

Auto Insurance: Required Before You Drive

Every state requires auto insurance to legally drive. Minimum requirements vary, but liability coverage is the baseline. Compare quotes at Progressive.com, Geico.com, or through a local independent agent. Without a US driving history, your first-year premiums will be higher — typically $100–$200/month depending on coverage and vehicle.

The Knowledge Test Is the Easy Part

Study the handbook, take 3–4 practice tests online until you are consistently getting 90%+, and you will pass on your first attempt. Most people find the actual driving test straightforward as long as they follow basic rules and do not panic. Schedule it, show up, and drive the way you would if a police officer were watching.
