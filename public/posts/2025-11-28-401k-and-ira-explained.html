401(k) and IRA Explained

Retirement accounts in the US offer powerful tax advantages that can save you tens of thousands of dollars over a career. Understanding how a 401(k) and IRA work — and using them — is one of the best financial moves you can make as a professional in America.

The 401(k): Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plan

A 401(k) is offered through your employer. You elect a percentage of each paycheck to contribute, and the money is invested in mutual funds or index funds you choose from a menu provided by your plan administrator.

Employer Match — Free Money: Many employers match a portion of your contributions. For example, "100% match up to 4% of salary" means if you earn $80,000 and contribute 4% ($3,200), your employer adds another $3,200. Always contribute at least enough to get the full match.

2024 contribution limit: $23,000 per year ($30,500 if you are age 50 or older).

Traditional vs. Roth: The Key Difference

Both 401(k)s and IRAs come in two flavors — Traditional and Roth. The difference is when you pay taxes:

Traditional

Contributions are pre-tax — reduces your taxable income today. You pay taxes when you withdraw money in retirement. Best when you expect to be in a lower tax bracket later.

Roth

Contributions are after-tax — no deduction today. But all growth and withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. Best when you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.

General rule: If you are early in your career with a lower income, lean toward Roth — you are paying taxes at a low rate now and getting tax-free growth for 30+ years. If you are a high earner, Traditional can make more sense.

The IRA: Individual Retirement Account

An IRA is opened independently by you — not through an employer — at any brokerage like Fidelity, Vanguard, or Schwab. It gives you more investment choices and you keep it regardless of where you work.

2024 contribution limit: $7,000 per year ($8,000 if age 50+).

Roth IRA income limits: You cannot contribute to a Roth IRA if your income is above $161,000 (single) or $240,000 (married filing jointly) in 2024. Traditional IRA has no income limit but deductibility may be restricted if you also have a 401(k).

Vesting: When Employer Contributions Are Truly Yours

Your own contributions are always 100% yours immediately. But employer matching contributions often have a vesting schedule — you only keep them if you stay with the company long enough.

  • Immediate vesting — employer match is yours from day one
  • Cliff vesting — 0% until a specific year, then 100% (e.g., nothing until year 3, then all of it)
  • Graded vesting — a portion unlocks each year (e.g., 20% per year over 5 years)

Leaving a Job: Rollover Your 401(k)

When you leave an employer, you have options for your 401(k):

  • Roll over to an IRA — most flexible, full control over investments (recommended)
  • Roll over to new employer's 401(k) — if the new plan has good funds
  • Leave it in the old plan — allowed if balance is over $5,000, but you lose track easily
  • Cash out — worst option: you pay income tax + a 10% early withdrawal penalty if under age 59.5

Start Now, Even Small

The biggest retirement savings mistake is waiting. Starting at 25 vs. 35 can mean the difference of hundreds of thousands of dollars at retirement thanks to compound growth. Contribute enough to get the full employer match first, then work toward maxing your Roth IRA.
