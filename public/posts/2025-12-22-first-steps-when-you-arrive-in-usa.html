First Steps When You Arrive in the USA

The first month in the US can feel overwhelming — there is so much to set up at once. This checklist walks you through every essential step in priority order, so nothing important falls through the cracks.

Week 1: The Non-Negotiables

Get a SIM Card

You need a US phone number immediately. Prepaid options: T-Mobile, Mint Mobile, or Cricket Wireless. No credit check, starts at $15–$30/month. Upgrade to a postpaid plan later once you have credit history.

Open a Bank Account

Do this in your first week. You need it to receive your paycheck and pay rent. Chime works without an SSN. See the separate bank account article for details.

Apply for Your SSN

Visit your local Social Security Administration (SSA) office. Bring your passport, visa, and immigration documents. Processing takes 2–4 weeks. You need this for employment, taxes, and most financial accounts.

Weeks 2–4: Build Your Foundation

Get a State-Issued ID or Driver's License

Visit your state's DMV (Department of Motor Vehicles). Bring your passport, visa, proof of US address (lease, utility bill), and SSN if you have it. A state ID is easier to get than a driver's license if you are not ready to take the driving tests. Either works as a domestic ID for daily use.

Get an ITIN if You Are Not Eligible for an SSN

If your visa type does not authorize work (student visa, certain dependent visas), you may still need to file taxes. Apply for an ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) by submitting Form W-7 to the IRS. Processing takes 7–11 weeks, so apply early.

Set Up Utilities

Electricity, gas, and water may come with your apartment or need to be set up separately. Internet is usually separate — compare Xfinity, AT&T, Spectrum, and local options. Without a credit history, some utility companies require a security deposit ($100–$200) — this is normal and returned when you close the account.

Set Up USPS Mail Forwarding / PO Box

Your bank cards, government documents, and insurance cards will all arrive by mail. If you are at a temporary address, set up mail forwarding at USPS.com or rent a PO Box at your local post office.

Important Places to Locate Near You

  • Social Security Administration (SSA) office — for SSN, benefit questions
  • DMV (Department of Motor Vehicles) — for license and state ID
  • Nearest urgent care / ER — know where this is before you need it
  • Public library — free internet, computers, notary services, and community programs
  • Post office — certified mail, passports, money orders

Emergency Numbers to Know

  • 911 — police, fire, or medical emergency (always free from any phone)
  • 311 — non-emergency city services (noise complaints, potholes, local info) — available in many cities
  • 211 — social services, food assistance, housing help — nationwide
  • Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222

Helpful Apps to Download

  • Google Maps — navigation, transit, walking directions
  • Uber / Lyft — rideshare, essential before you have a car
  • Zelle / Venmo / Cash App — send money to people in the US instantly
  • Your bank's app — mobile check deposit, transfers, account management
  • IRS2Go — official IRS app for tax refund tracking
  • Nextdoor — neighborhood app for local info, recommendations, and community events

One Step at a Time

Do not try to do everything on day one. Prioritize: phone, bank account, SSN application. Everything else follows. Within a month you will have the foundation in place and the US will start feeling a lot more navigable.
