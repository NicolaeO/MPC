Renting Your First Apartment in the USA

Finding and securing your first apartment in the US involves more steps than in most other countries — credit checks, background screenings, proof of income, and upfront costs that can add up to several months of rent before you even move in. Here is exactly what to expect.

Where to Search for Apartments

  • Zillow.com / Trulia.com — largest listing sites, great for browsing and getting a sense of the market
  • Apartments.com — dedicated rental site with detailed filters
  • Zumper.com — good for finding no-fee apartments and comparing neighborhoods
  • Facebook Marketplace — often has private landlord listings with less formality and no broker fee
  • Craigslist.org — still widely used, but be cautious of scams (never send money before viewing)
  • Community groups — Moldovan / Eastern European Facebook groups in your city often have tips and subletting opportunities

What Landlords Check

Credit Score

Most landlords want 620–680 minimum. If you have no credit history, offer to pay 2–3 months upfront or bring a co-signer with good credit.

Income

Standard requirement is gross income of at least 3x the monthly rent. For a $1,500/month apartment, you need to show $4,500/month income. Provide recent pay stubs or an offer letter.

Background Check

Criminal history check. Minor or old offenses may not be disqualifying. Be honest on applications — dishonesty is more likely to get you rejected than the actual record.

Upfront Costs to Budget For

  • Application fee — $35–$75, usually non-refundable, covers the credit and background check
  • Security deposit — typically 1–2 months rent; returned when you move out if no damage beyond normal wear
  • First month's rent — always due upfront
  • Last month's rent — some landlords require this too, effectively 3× rent due on signing
  • Broker fee — in some cities (New York especially), a broker's fee of 1 month's rent is common. Many online listings have no broker fee.
Budget tip: Have at least 3 months of rent in cash ready before you start apartment hunting. You may need security deposit + first + last month all at once.

Reading Your Lease

A lease is a legally binding contract. Read it fully before signing. Key things to check:

  • Lease term (12 months standard, some month-to-month)
  • What utilities are included (water, gas, electricity, trash, internet)
  • Pet policy and fees
  • Rules on subletting or guests staying long-term
  • Early termination clause — what happens if you need to break the lease
  • Rent increase policy at lease renewal
  • Notice period required to vacate (typically 30–60 days)

Moving In: Protect Your Deposit

On move-in day, do a thorough walkthrough and document every pre-existing issue with timestamped photos — scratches on floors, stains on carpet, holes in walls, anything that was there before you moved in. Email these photos to your landlord within 24 hours and keep a copy. This protects you when you move out.

Renters Insurance

Renters insurance costs about $15–$25 per month and covers your belongings in case of theft, fire, or water damage — and protects you from liability if someone is injured in your apartment. Many landlords now require it. Lemonade.com is a popular option with quick setup.

No Credit History? You Still Have Options

If you have no credit history, look for private landlords (vs. property management companies) who have more flexibility. Offer a larger upfront payment, bring a co-signer, or show bank statements with strong savings. Some newer platforms like Jetty offer lease guarantor services for a fee.
