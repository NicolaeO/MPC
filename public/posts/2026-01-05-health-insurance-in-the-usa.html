Health Insurance in the USA

Healthcare in the US is expensive without insurance — a single ER visit can cost $3,000–$10,000, and a surgery can run $50,000 or more. Understanding how health insurance works is not optional; it is one of the most important financial topics for anyone living in America.

Key Terms You Must Know

Premium

The monthly amount you pay for coverage, whether or not you use any healthcare. Think of it like a subscription fee. Employer plans often subsidize most of this cost.

Deductible

The amount you pay out of pocket each year before insurance starts covering costs. A $2,000 deductible means you pay the first $2,000 of medical bills yourself annually.

Copay

A fixed dollar amount you pay per visit or prescription. For example, $25 for a primary care visit, $50 for a specialist, $10 for generic medications. Often applies before the deductible is met.

Coinsurance

After you meet your deductible, you and the insurance company split remaining costs. A typical 80/20 plan means insurance pays 80%, you pay 20% of each bill.

Out-of-Pocket Max

The most you will ever pay in a year. Once reached, insurance covers 100% of covered services. In 2024, the maximum allowed is $9,450 for an individual plan.

Network

Doctors and hospitals that have contracted with your insurance company for discounted rates. Seeing in-network providers costs significantly less than out-of-network.

Plan Types

  • HMO (Health Maintenance Organization) — must use in-network providers; need a referral from your primary care doctor to see a specialist; lower premiums. Good if you want predictable, lower costs.
  • PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) — can see any doctor without a referral; out-of-network allowed (higher cost); higher premiums. Good if you want flexibility.
  • EPO (Exclusive Provider Organization) — in-network only like HMO, but no referral needed like PPO; mid-range premiums.
  • HDHP (High-Deductible Health Plan) — low monthly premium, high deductible ($1,600+ individual). The only plan type that qualifies you for an HSA. Good if you are healthy and want to save tax-free for healthcare.

Where to Get Health Insurance

Through Your Employer (Most Common)

Most medium and large employers offer health insurance as a benefit and pay a significant portion of the premium (often 60–80%). This is almost always the best option when available. You can enroll during your company's open enrollment period, or within 30–60 days of starting a new job.

Healthcare.gov Marketplace

If your employer does not offer coverage or you are self-employed, shop on the federal marketplace at Healthcare.gov. Open Enrollment runs November 1 – January 15 each year. Outside of this window, you need a qualifying life event (job loss, marriage, new child, move) to enroll. Income-based subsidies (Premium Tax Credits) can significantly reduce your cost.

Medicaid

Free or very low-cost coverage for people with low income. Eligibility varies by state — in most states, a single adult earning under ~$20,120/year qualifies. Check your state's Medicaid program.

COBRA

If you lose employer coverage (job loss, leaving a job), COBRA lets you continue the same plan for up to 18 months. The catch: you pay the full premium — including the employer's share — which is typically very expensive ($500–$700+/month for an individual).

Tip: Before any planned procedure or specialist visit, call your insurance company and verify that the provider is in-network AND that the specific procedure is covered. Getting surprised by out-of-network bills is one of the most common healthcare financial mistakes in the US.

Get Coverage Before You Need It

You cannot predict when you will get sick or injured. Health insurance is not optional in the US — the financial risk of being uninsured is too high. Even a basic plan with a high deductible is better than no coverage at all. Enroll as soon as you are eligible.
