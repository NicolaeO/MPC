Understanding Your Paycheck

Your first US paycheck can be confusing — you earn $4,000 a month but only $2,800 lands in your account. Here is a breakdown of every deduction and why it exists, so you know exactly where your money goes.

Gross Pay vs. Net Pay

Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions — the number in your job offer letter. Net pay (or "take-home pay") is what actually hits your bank account after all deductions are subtracted. The gap between the two typically ranges from 20% to 35% depending on your income and benefits elections.

Pre-Tax Deductions (Reduce Your Taxable Income)

These are taken out before taxes are calculated, which lowers the amount of income you pay taxes on. Contributing more to these is generally beneficial.

  • 401(k) contributions — retirement savings; reduces federal and state taxable income
  • Health insurance premium — your share of the employer health plan; usually pre-tax
  • HSA contributions — health savings account; triple tax advantage
  • FSA contributions — flexible spending account for medical or childcare costs
  • Dental and vision insurance — usually small amounts, also pre-tax

FICA Taxes (Social Security & Medicare)

FICA stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. These fund Social Security and Medicare and are automatically deducted from every paycheck:

  • Social Security: 6.2% of wages up to $168,600 (2024 wage base)
  • Medicare: 1.45% of all wages (plus an extra 0.9% if you earn over $200,000)
  • Your employer matches these amounts — so the total contribution is 15.3%, split evenly
Note: If you are on certain visa types (F-1, J-1, etc.) you may be exempt from FICA for a period. Check with your employer's payroll department.

Federal Income Tax Withholding

Your employer withholds federal income tax based on the information you provided on your W-4 form when you were hired. The W-4 tells the employer how much to withhold each pay period. If too much is withheld, you get a refund in April. If too little is withheld, you owe money.

You can update your W-4 at any time through HR. The IRS has a free Tax Withholding Estimator at irs.gov to help you get it right.

State Income Tax

Most states also withhold state income tax. Rates vary widely — Illinois is a flat 4.95%, California goes up to 13.3%, while Texas, Florida, and a few others have no state income tax at all.

Reading Your Pay Stub

Your pay stub (paper or digital) shows a breakdown of every line item. Look for these columns:

  • Current — amount for this pay period
  • YTD (Year-to-Date) — total accumulated since January 1st
  • Earnings section — regular pay, overtime, bonuses
  • Deductions section — taxes, benefits, and any garnishments

Pay Frequency Types

  • Weekly — 52 paychecks per year
  • Bi-weekly — every two weeks, 26 paychecks per year (most common in tech)
  • Semi-monthly — twice per month on fixed dates (e.g., 1st and 15th), 24 paychecks
  • Monthly — 12 paychecks per year (less common)

Quick Rule of Thumb

In most states, budget for taking home roughly 70–75% of your gross salary. If you live in a high-tax state like California or New York, budget closer to 65%. Use the extra clarity to plan your budget from day one.
