How to Find a Job in the USA

The US job market operates differently from most countries — networking matters more than anywhere else, resumes follow a specific format, and most jobs are filled before they are even publicly posted. Here is how to navigate it effectively as a professional and newcomer.

The US Resume Format

A US resume is different from a European CV. Key rules:

  • One page — for most people with under 10 years of experience. Two pages for very senior professionals.
  • No photo, no age, no marital status — these are not included in US resumes (anti-discrimination laws).
  • Reverse chronological — most recent job first.
  • Bullet points with accomplishments — not just job duties. "Reduced API response time by 40%" beats "Responsible for optimizing APIs".
  • Quantify everything possible — numbers, percentages, team sizes, revenue, users.
  • Contact info — name, phone, email, LinkedIn URL, city/state (no full address needed).
ATS (Applicant Tracking System): Most medium and large companies use software to filter resumes before a human ever sees them. Use keywords directly from the job posting. Plain formatting works best — avoid tables, columns, and graphics in the resume file.

LinkedIn: Your Most Important Tool

LinkedIn is used far more seriously in the US than in most other countries. Recruiters actively search it and will contact you if your profile is complete.

  • Professional photo — clear, good lighting, business or business-casual attire
  • Headline — your current role or the role you are targeting (not just "Looking for work")
  • About section — 2–3 paragraphs on your experience, skills, and what you bring
  • All work experience filled in — same content as your resume
  • Skills section — add specific technologies, tools, and skills relevant to your field
  • Connect actively — connect with colleagues, classmates, people you meet. 500+ connections dramatically increases your visibility.
  • Turn on "Open to Work" — signals recruiters you are looking (can be visible only to recruiters, not your employer)

Where to Apply

LinkedIn Jobs

The largest professional job board. Apply directly or use Easy Apply. More important: connect with the hiring manager or recruiter before or after applying — it dramatically increases response rates.

Indeed

Aggregates job postings from all over the web. Great for broad searches and volume applying. Set up email alerts for your target role and location.

Company Careers Pages

Apply directly on the company's website when possible — these applications often get more attention than third-party submissions. Research the company beforehand.

Networking: The Most Effective Strategy

Studies consistently show that 70–80% of jobs are filled through networking — through someone knowing someone. Cold applications alone have a low response rate. Here is how to build your network:

  • Attend industry meetups and conferences (Meetup.com, Eventbrite, local tech events)
  • Join professional associations in your field
  • Reach out to alumni from your university who work at your target companies
  • Ask for informational interviews — 20-minute calls to learn about someone's role and company, not to ask for a job directly
  • Let former colleagues and managers know you are looking — a referral from inside a company can skip you past the ATS entirely

The Interview Process

  • Phone/Video screen — 30-minute call with a recruiter, mostly about your background and basic fit
  • Technical or skills interview — for tech roles, coding challenges; for other roles, case studies or work samples
  • Panel interview — meeting with 2–5 team members, often behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time when...")
  • Offer call — verbal offer, followed by written offer letter

Salary Research and Negotiation

Always research market rates before interviewing. Resources: Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (tech), LinkedIn Salary, Payscale. The first offer is usually not the final offer — negotiating is expected and will not hurt your chances if done professionally. A simple "I am very excited about this opportunity. Based on my research of the market, I was expecting something closer to X — is there flexibility?" is enough.

Work Authorization

On job applications, you will be asked if you are authorized to work in the US. If you have a work visa, EAD, or green card — answer yes. You will also be asked if you require sponsorship — answer honestly. Many companies sponsor H-1B visas for skilled professionals; some do not. Targeting companies with sponsorship history (found on myvisajobs.com) saves time.
